Amazing things to see during your Dubrovnik holidays

Pearl of Adriatic, more commonly known as Dubrovnik, is most astonishing tourist attraction in Croatia. Situated in Dalmatia on the coast of cerulean and warm Adriatic Sea, is a perfect destiny for a city break.

Walls of Dubrovnik and city's stunning monuments with examples of Gothic, Renaissance and Baroque architecture, made Dubrovnik one of UNESCO’s World Heritage Sites. You’ve probably already seen on your television screen Stari Grad, city’s old town with its marvelous view of red terracotta rooftops on limestone buildings, majestic defensive towers and Dubrovnik’s port. City was the main filming location for King’s Landing in the Game of Thrones series, so whether you’re a great fan of George Martin's novel screen adaptation or an art and history admirer, you’ve just found an ideal place for your holiday.

Brief History

The origins of the settlement date back to the seventh century, when Roman citizens of nearby city Epidaurum took shelter on Laus Island, fleeing from Slavs and Avars. Refugees built their settlement on a rocky island in the southern part of present Stari Grad and named it Ragusium. That is one of theories and one that was seriously undermined by the archeological excavations, conducted beneath city’s cathedral in 1981. Studies have shown that discovered remains originate from seventh century and that they might be remains of Dubrovnik’s first cathedral. In that case, Laus Island had to be well settled long before the Epidaurum citizens arrived. As city’s genesis is debatable, it is known for sure that in tenth century on the main land just opposite Ragusium, Slavic Croats established Dubrava. Following peaceful coexistence during next centuries, two settlements finally became one. In twelfth century a narrow channel dividing both towns was buried, joining Ragusium with Dubrava and at the same time forming Dubrovnik’s main street – Stradun.

In the Middle Ages, Dubrovnik belonged to or was dependent on different neighbouring states. Starting from Byzantine Empire, the Normans, Croatian Kingdom, Venetian Republic, Hungarian Kingdom and finally Ottoman Empire. Although not independent, Dubrovnik together with surrounding areas enjoyed some autonomy. In 1358, based on the agreement with Hungarian King Luis the Great, city’s nobles managed to form a maritime city state named Republic of Ragusa. For annual tribute and help with its fleet, Republic gained the right to self-government. Some hundred years later, the city changed its suzerainty to the Ottoman Empire, but still kept its autonomy and continued to flourish as a maritime trade power. With the end of sixteenth century Dubrovnik’s position, together with other maritime republics, began to deteriorate. Due to great discoveries in previous centuries, Mediterranean trade routes lost their economic importance. Falling into poverty city was additionally struck with a catastrophe. On the 6th of April 1667 a devastating earthquake razed Dubrovnik, burying thousands of its citizens.

Republic of Ragusa fell in 1808 when Napoleon annexed Dubrovnik to Napoleonic Kingdom of Italy and then to French Empire. 7 years later, after Napoleon’s defeat and Congress of Vienna, former republic’s territory was incorporated into the Austrian Empire. By the end of the Great War and the fall of Austro-Hungarian Monarchy, Dubrovnik became a part of the Kingdom of Yugoslavia and after the II World War, found itself in the communist state of Yugoslavia. On the 25th of June 1991 Croats declared their independence, causing the outbreak of war with the Serbs. During this conflict Dubrovnik was besieged for seven months and suffered heavy artillery shelling. The Croatian War of Independence ended in 1995 with Dubrovnik remaining within Croatian borders.

Dubrovnik Historical Sites

Gate of Pile and Placa

Most of city’s important monuments are located within Stari Grad. The best place to start your Dubrovnik walking tour is the Gate of Pile, most impressive entrance to old town, located on the west side of the settlement. The road to the gate leads above the gardens, which previously were a moat, through stone bridge and a wooden drawbridge. Nowadays stone bridge was built in 1471 during the reconstructions and enlargement of the moat by the design of Paskoje Milicevic, the first one was built in 1397 by Ivan of Siena. The drawbridge was added in 1537, together with the Renaissance Outer Gate, which we can admire today. A statue of city’s patron, Saint Blaise, is carved upon the arch of Dubrovnik’s western entrance. Passing the Outer Gate you will find yourself in front of Gothic Inner Gate, dated back to 1460 with a similar statue upone the arch.

Dubrovnik’s main street - Placa or Stradun - begins just behind the western gates and marks the early medieval channel separating Roman settlers of the Laus Island on the south from Slavic Croats on the mainland. Present, Baroque appearance of Placa as well as the rest of the town is a consequence of a catastrophic earthquake in 1667, which demolished nearly all Gothic and Renaissance tenements.

Great Onofrio’s Fountain

Placa, just behind the Inner Pile Gate, starts with the Great Onofrio’s Fountain, a roundish water container crowned with a cupola. Fountain was built in 1440 and was one of the final points of city’s aqueduct system. Reservoir designed and build by a Neapolitan named Onofrio Giordano della Cava has 16 unique stone masks reliefs floating with water. Unfortunately nowadays fountain doesn’t have the original appearance as it suffered from the earthquake.

Saint Saviour Church

Opposite the fountain stands Saint Saviour Church, a small Renaissance votive sanctuary. Church was built in 1528 by the design of Petar Andrijic, famous architect from Korcula Island. Construction of the temple was commissioned by city’s council after an earthquake in 1520 as a token of gratitude for saving the city from destruction. Church is a bit of a style mixture, it has Gothic cross-ribbed ceiling and narrow, Gothic-styled windows on both sides but the main facade with its three-leaf semicircular top and Antique-styled portal is typical for Renaissance. Saint Saviour Church remained unaffected by the disastrous earthquake from 1667 so we can admire it in its original form.

Franciscan Monastery

Passing Saint Saviour Church down the Stradun, you will notice a portal with an exceptional sculpture of Pieta above it. It’s side entrance to Franciscan Church and Monastery. First Franciscan Monastery was built in thirteenth century west of Pile Gate but due to constant war threats a decision was made to build a new one, inside the city. Construction of the new monastery started in 1317, but it lasted for many years. Monastery Church suffered from the earthquake of 1667, the only element of original structure that survived, is mentioned above tympanum with Pieta. Gothic sculpture was carved in 1498 by local artists, brothers Leonard and Petar Petrovic. Central figures of Virgin Mary and Jesus are accompanied by the sculptures of God Almighty at the top, Saint Jerome on left and Saint John the Baptist on the right side.

Monastery buildings stretch north from the Church, up to Minceta Tower and they are divided into two parts, with only one available for public. The southern, accessible cloister built in 1360 by Mihoje Brajkov, is a stunning example of late Romanesque architecture. Designed as a garden for growing herbs used to create medicaments, cloister draws attention with its delightful decoration. Each capital of cloister’s columns is an unique carving of human, animal or floral motifs. Ground floor of Franciscan Monastery includes also one of the oldest, still functioning pharmacies in the world. It was established in 1317, so in the same year the Order moved from outside the city walls. Monastery is also known for its remarkable library with over thousand of old manuscripts.

Sponza Palace

Heading further east at the end of the Stradun street, you’ll find yourself in front of Sponza Palace on Luza Squar. The Palace is one of Dubrovnik’s few buildings that survived in their original shape the massive earthquake of 1667. Built between 1516 and 1522 by design of Paskoje Milicevic, construction is a perfect example of Gothic and Renaissance mixtures. Ground floor with its portico and atrium represents Renaissance style, which then is disturbed on the first floor by Gothic influenced windows and again occur on the second floor, in the shape of Renaissance styled windows and a statue of Saint Blaise in the centre. Sponza Palace had a number of different public functions. Since its beginnings it used to be the seat of city’s officials but also a mint, armoury, bank and a treasury. Nowadays it’s a home to State Archives.

Orlando’s Column

In the central point of Luza Square stands a statue of medieval armoured knight holding his sword, known as Orlando's Column. According to myth, during the Saracen invasion in ninth century Orlando, better known across whole Europe as Roland, led his fleet to save Dubrovnik and broke the siege of the city. The column is supposed to be an expression of gratitude from the citizens. In fact, Roland-like statues were erected in many European, mostly German cities in Middle Ages as a symbol of city’s freedom and rights as a town.

Orlando’s Column and Saint Blaise Church

Orlando’s Column

Orlando’s Column was founded by Sigmund of Luxemburg, King of Hungary and Croatia and erected in 1419 by design of Bonino di Milano and carving of Antun Dubrovcanin. Column also served for few other than decorative purposes. Orlando’s right forearm was an official measure of length known as dubrovacki lakat or Ragusan cubit (51,2 cm or 1,7 feet). Platform on the top of the column was used for public proclamations and the area around it was a common place to execute punishments. Today, Orlando’s Column still serves the citizens as a central point for Dubrovnik’s Summer Festival ceremonies.

City Bell Tower

On the east side of Luza Square stands the City Bell Tower or rather its faithful reconstruction from 1929 as the tower started to lean after the earthquake in 1667. The original tower was built in 1444 by three local architects. Bronze statues at the top, the famous Zelenci meaning green people, strike the tower’s bell every hour. Original statues were made from wood and the original bell was replaced with present one in 1509. Apart from current time, brass face of the City Bell Tower shows moon phases as well.

Just right to the tower stands House of the Main Guard, former residence of the Ragusa Republic Admiral. Building was constructed in 1490 although the portal was added at the beginning of eighteenth century by Venetian architect, Marino Gropelli. Next to the City Guard building stands another of Onofrio’s della Cava works - Little Onofrio’s Fountain. Although smaller reservoir is by Onofrio’s design, it was carved between 1440 and 1442 by Milanese sculptor Petar Martinov.

Church of Saint Blaise

On the south side of Luza Square stands Dubrovnik’s most popular temple - Church of Saint Blaise. The church stands in place of first Romanesque one, built in 1368 in the honour of city’s patron saint. The first temple suffered of the earthquake from 1667 and what remained was burned down during the fire in 1706. Present day church was erected between 1706 and 1715 by Venetian architect and sculptor Marino Gropelli. Baroque building is inspired by San Maurizio Church in Venice but is definitely more ornamented than its archetype. Main entrance with beautifully decorated tympanum on front façade can be reached by a wide staircase. Temple is crowned with a cupola and three statues standing on facade’s balustrade with Saint Blaise atop.

Interior of this single nave temple is richly decorated with Baroque styled components but the central element of the main altar is gold plated, Gothic statue of Saint Blaise. Figure of city’s patron was made from silver in fifteenth century by unknown artist and is one of the few elements from old church, surviving both earthquake and fire. Statue is also important as thanks to the model of Dubrovnik held by Saint Blaise, we can see how city looked like before the earthquake. The saint himself, who lived in fourth century, became a patron of the city in 972. According to the legend, he warned Dubrovnik’s citizens of an Venetian attack. Since then, he became city’s protector and grateful citizens arrange a feast in his honour each year on the 3rd of February.

Rector’s Palace

Heading south from Saint Blaise Church via Pred Dvorom street you’ll reach Rector’s Palace, an example of secular architecture masterpiece. At times of Ragusan Republic building was the seat to city’s Rector, governor elected for one month. Palace was destroyed numerous times and each time rebuilt by different architect with different conception and style. Nowadays palace is an unique and harmonic mixture of Gothic, Renaissance and Baroque architecture. Rector’s Palace stands in place of medieval fortress, first mention of building is from thirteenth century. Building was destroyed in 1435 by a fire and the city council assigned Onofrio della Cava to rebuild it. Architect erected the palace raising a two storey, Gothic structure with portico, atrium and two towers. Columns of the entrance porch with their richly decorated capitals were carved by Pietro di Martino from Milan. Unlike the towers, carvings on two of these columns can still be admired.

Palace was destroyed again in 1463 by a massive gunpowder explosion and the task to rebuild it was given to Michelozzo di Bartolomeo Michelozzi from Florence. Florentine ideas met with lack of understanding from the city council and job was handed to local architects. Portico’s columns were reshaped in Renaissance style by another Florentine Salvi di Michele, portal was made by Pavko Antoje Bogicevic and second floor windows by Ragusan Radivoj Bogosalic and Nikola Markovic. In 1638 a statue of Dubrovnik’s most meritorious citizen, Miho Pracat, was placed in atrium. Miho was a rich ship-owner who left his fortune to Ragusan Republic. Statue was carved by Pietro Giacometti. Building was again struck by disastrous earthquakes in 1520 and 1667. With next reconstruction, this time with Baroque-styled elements, atrium's staircase was rebuilt and a Baroque bell was added on top of the second floor. Today Rector’s Palace is the seat of Dubrovnik Museum with numerous exhibitions depicting city’s history and life of its citizens.

Cathedral of the Assumption of the Virgin Mary

A bit further south from Rector’s Palace stands Dubrovnik Cathedral of the Assumption of the Virgin Mary, probably a third one in city’s history. Remains of the first temple were found during archaeological excavations in 1981 and studies have shown that they originate from seventh century. This remarkable discovery could undermine the theory of city’s foundation by the Roman refugees from Epidaurum in seventh century. If these are indeed remains of the first cathedral, then this area had to be inhabited long before the Romans came and had to be quite flourishing if its citizens could afford a huge temple. As it is not known if the first sacral building was in fact a cathedral and what exactly happened to it, we know that the second one was the cathedral and was founded between twelfth and fourteenth century. Legends say that its founder was English King, Richard the Lion Heart, who shipwrecked on the nearby Lokrum Island while returning from the Third Crusade in 1192. As a token of gratitude for surviving the storm, Richard swore to build a great church in the place of his rescue. Romanesque cathedral was completely destroyed during the earthquake of 1667. Reconstructions lasted till 1713 and although first design was by Italian Andrea Bufalini, the final shape of Roman-Baroque cathedral was the result of the efforts of several other foreign and local architects. Frontal facade of this three nave temple is decorated with Corinthian columns and pilasters, one main and two side portals with plain tympanums and two niches with statues of saints on both wings of the building. Second storey is crowned with a wide and plain tympanum on top of a single window and a balustrade with statues of saints on both sides of it. Both north and south facades are less ornamented, with one small portal on each side but quite big, semicircular windows above them.

Temple’s interior consists of one main and two side naves, separated with two rows of columns. Intersection of main nave and transept is illuminated by copula’s windows above it. The main altar impresses with its sixteenth century polyptych by great Venetian painter Tiziano Veceli. His stunning work, Ascension of Mary, was originally ordered by Order of Saint Lazarus and placed in its church on Ploce district. Both the cathedral and Church of Saint Lazarus were completely demolished during the earthquake but as only the Cathedral was rebuilt, city’s council decided to move the painting there. Side naves are also decorated with beautiful Baroque altars such as Saint John’s, votive altar of Lady of the Port or Petilovrijenci altar. Other exquisite artifacts and art works are located in Cathedral’s Treasury. Most valuable for Dubrovnik citizens are the reliquaries of Saint Blaise, gold plated arm and his head, shaped into Byzantine imperial crown encrusted with precious stones. The Treasury holds also a vast number of other saint’s relics, liturgical vessels dated from thirteenth century, great art works such as the Romanesque-Byzantine icon of Madonna and Child from twelfth century and other paintings from Italian Renaissance masters like Palma il Giovane or Girolamo Savoldo. It’s worth to know that although the Treasury was located in the Cathedral, it was property of the Republic, not the church. Access to the Treasury was possible only through the simultaneous use of three different keys held by the archbishop, cathedral’s rector and secretary of Ragusan Republic.

Walls of Dubrovnik

From its beginnings, city was surrounded by defensive walls, which were constantly widened and raised. Magnificent, limestone walls of Dubrovnik which we can admire today, were erected mainly from fourteenth till the second half of sixteenth century and now stretch for nearly 2 kilometers (6365 feet). Walls are 4 to 6 meters (13 – 20 feet) thick on the north - land side, 1,5 to 3 meters (5 – 10 feet) on the south - sea side and 25 meters (83 feet) in its highest parts. Apart from the impregnable walls, city’s fortification system consisted of number of towers, bastions, forts, bulwarks and a moat.

First fortifications were surrounding the pre-Dubrovnik settlement on the Laus Island and were probably a wooden palisade. Still, they had to be very solid and impressive if the settlement survived a 15 month long Saracen siege in ninth century. Town’s borders evolved during the next centuries, when citizens expanded east from the todays Cathedral, settling whole Laus and then, in eleventh century, north beyond the sea channel joining the Croatian Dubrava. Merging these two settlements required also building a single wall around the newly established Dubrovnik, which finally was enclosed in thirteenth century.

Bokar, Kula and Minceta Towers

If you begin your City Walls tour from the south west corner of the fortification, your starting point will be the Bokar Fortress. Fort’s construction begun in 1461 by the design of Florentine architect, Michelozzo di Bartololmeo but wasn’t finished till 1570. This round shaped casamatta was constructed in front of the walls but connected with a passage. Fort Bokar was of most high importance as it defended both Pila Gate and western port – Kolorina, from which nowadays you can sail out with a canoe or a boat to admire Dubrovnik’s City Walls from the sea side. Continuing further north the Wall you will come across Kula, meaning tower, Puncjela. Construction of this square tower begun in 1305 but continued till 1350, then in 1463 was reinforced with a new, thicker wall. Tower dominates over the Convent of Saint Claire, designated for girls from noble families and actually took its name from it, as Puncjela derives from early Italian pulzella, meaning 'girls'. On the outside, upper part of the tower a sculpture of Saint Blaise is carved.

Going further, pass the Pila Gate to the north west corner of the City Walls, you’ll find yourself in front of Minceta Tower. Todays huge, round fort was built in 1319 by Nicifor Ranjina and originally was square shaped. Tower was rebuilt and reinforced due to Turkish threat after the fall of Constantinople. Reconstruction begun in 1461 by the design of Michelozzo di Bartololmeo who ordered to encircle former square fort with thicker, round walls and joined it with low scarp walls system. Michelozzo left Dubrovnik because of an insult he suffered from the council and the constructions were continued by Giorgio da Sebenico from Zadar, who added, in rather ornamental manner, Gothic battlements at the top of the fort. Minceta Tower was completed in 1464 and with its 9 guns became an impenetrable obstacle in fifteenth century and a perfect viewpoint for today’s tourists.

Ploce and Buza Gates

The northern part of the city fortifications consists of thick walls upon a huge scarp, with six towers and one gate. The northern entrance named Buza Gate was added during the Austrian reign in 1908. The Gate was carved in the wall to make it easier for the officers to access the tennis courts. The north east corner of the City Walls were protected by the Revelin Fortress and Asimon Tower. The tower was built in the fourteenth century, when the Dominican Monastery was included into the city. Right below the tower, an eastern entrance to Dubrovnik was constructed at the same time.

Inner Ploce Gate

Outer Ploce Gate

The original, inner Ploce Gate is a Romanesque archway, with a relief of Saint Blaise’s head, right next to it, the Austrians carved another, wider one, at the end of nineteenth century. Above the outer archway of the gate, just as in the Pila Gate, stands a statue of Saint Blaise and the bridge leading the way to Revelin Fortress is very similar to the one in front of western gate, as both were designed and constructed in fifteenth century by Paskoje Milicevic.

Revelin Fortress

Revelin Fortress, the greatest Dubrovnik’s fortification, is located east from the City Walls. First fort, much less impressive, was built in 1463 as a result of Constantinople’s fall in 1453. The Ottoman Empire threat and constant rivalry with the Venetians, led the city council to decision to strengthen the fortress. This task was entrusted by Dubrovnik’s officials to a military architect, Antonio Ferramolino from Bergamo. Construction began in 1538 and due to its priority, was already finished in 1549. Ferramolino constructed an irregular, quadrilateral fort, which was once protected by the harbour from the south and surrounded by the moat on the other sides.

With one bridge connecting it to Ploca Gate and second on the opposite side, Revelin became an impregnable fortress. Not only was it invincible but also extremely solid. Revelin was one of the few constructions in Dubrovnik, that survived the devastating earthquake in 1667 and as a result became an administrative centre for its officials and a treasury for its wealth.

Saint John Fortress

On the opposite side of the Old Port, on the south east corner of City Walls stands the Saint John's Fortress locally called Tvrdava Svetog Ivana. Shape of todays fortress is the effect of a number of upgrades of two separate forts and finall merging them into one. It consists of Dock Fort which construction began in 1346 and a bit younger, Fort Gundulic which probably was raised in fifteenth century. Both forts were upgraded and reconstructed several times in sixteenth century and finally in 1552 city’s council decided to combine them. This task was given to well known Paskoje Milicevic, who completed the reconstructions in 1557.

Today, Fortress of Saint John is home to Maritime Museum with exhibits from glorious naval past of Dubrovnik and to city’s Aquarium where you can admire Adriatic sea life. The southern walls, although aren’t as thick as those which were protecting Dubrovnik from the land, stand on high, inaccessible cliffs. Additionally, city from the sea side was protected by five bastions which are: Saint Mary's Tower - Sveta Marija, Saint Peter's Tower - Tvrdava Mrtvo Zvono, Holy Marguerite's Tower - Sveta Margarita, Saint Etienne's Tower - Sveti Stjepan and Holy Saviour's Tower - Sveti Spasitelj.
